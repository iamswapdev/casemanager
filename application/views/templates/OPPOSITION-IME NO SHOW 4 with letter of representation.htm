This affirmation is submitted in opposition to Defendant's motion for Summary Judgment dismissing plaintiff’s complaint based on alleged outstanding verification requests.

 

THE DEFENDANT HAS NOT ESTABLISHED THAT PLAINTIFF’S ASSIGNOR WILLFULLY MISSED THE APPONTMENT

The defendant’s refusal to pay the plaintiff’s claim is based upon the allegation that the plaintiff’s assignor failed to attend an Independent Medical Examination (IME) and, therefore, breached a policy condition. The defendant, however, has failed to establish that there was a material breach of the policy. A claimant’s mere failure to attend and IME is not sufficient to establish that the policy was breached.

While it is not disputed that recovery of no-fault benefits can be denied upon a willful failure to attend an IME, the defendant has not established the plaintiff’s assignor failure to attend the IME was a willful failure. As recently held by the Supreme Court, New York County. In order to establish willful failure constituting a material breach of the policy, plaintiff must show that the defendant’s attitude ‘was one of willful and avowed obstruction.’ Involving a ‘pattern of noncooperation for which no reasonable excuse is offered’. Unitrin Advantage Inc. Co. v. Andrew Carothers, M.D., 17 Misc. 1121(A) (Supreme Court, New York County, August 20, 2007) quoting Ingarra v. General Acc./PG Ins. Co. of NY, 273  A.D. 2d 766 (3rd Dept. 2003). “The insurer’s burden of proving willfulness is a heavy one.” Id.

At bar, the defendant has not come close to proving this “high burden” that the plaintiff’s assignor’s failure to appear for the IME was willful. The defendant merely asserts that the plaintiff’s assignor failed to attend the IME, after IME request letters were mailed to him. The mere fact that the plaintiff’s assignors failed to submit to the IME , after allegedly being notified about the IME  via letter in no way demonstrates a willful failure to attend the IME. As there was no willful failure to attend the IME, there was no policy breach and any denial based on that assertion is invalid as a matter of law.

Furthermore, the defendant has failed to attach a copy of the policy to its motion. As such, the defendant has not established that the failure to appear for an IME is a policy violation. According to the defendant’s denial, the claim was denied “due to non-compliance of policy conditions” Without a copy of the policy, the defendant cannot establish that the alleged non-appearance was even a policy violation. Accordingly, the defendant has not met it burden for summary judgment.

 

THE DEFENDANT HAS FAILED TO ESTABLISH THE IME SCHEDULING LETTERS WERE MAILED TO THE PATIENT’S CURRENT ADDRESS

The defendant has failed to establish that the IME scheduling letters were even mailed to the correct address. According to the affidavit of Ronni McLaughlin, the first and second letter was mailed to 73 Bay 22nd St, Apt 1, Brooklyn, NY 11214.  According to the affidavit, the third letter was mailed to 73 Bay 22nd St, Apt A2, Brooklyn, NY 11214.  Note, the actual third scheduling letter attached shows that it was missing the apartment number altogether.  Also, the accident report,  annexed to the defendant’s moving papers, shows that the correct apartment number is #A2. Copies of these documents are annexed hereto as Exhibit A. The defendant has these documents, but apparently made no effort to ascertain the patient’s proper address. There are clearly questions of fact regarding the notice the patient had for IM. If the letters were, in fact mailed to the wrong address, the patient likely never received them. As such, the defendant’s denial for non –appearance would be improper. The burden is on the defendant to prove the letters were mailed to the proper address. The defendant has not met its burden. As question of fact exist regarding the patients address, the defendant has not met is burden for summary judgment.

Also, annexed to Exhibit A is an NF2 filed by the attorney.  Copies of the scheduling letters were never mailed to the attorney.

 

WHEREFORE, it is respectfully requested that the defendant’s motion be in all respects denied, together with such other, further and different relief this court may deem just and proper.
