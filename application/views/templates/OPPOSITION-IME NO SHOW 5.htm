Alek Beynenson, an attorney duly admitted to practice law before the Courts of the State of New York; hereby affirms the following to be true under the penalties of perjury:

I am a member of the law firm of The Beynenson Law Firm PC, attorneys of record for Plaintiff, and as such am fully, familiar with the facts and circumstances surrounding this action from the records and files maintained therein.

This affirmation is submitted in opposition to Defendant's cross-motion for Summary Judgment dismissing plaintiff’s complaint based on alleged lack of a valid cause of action.

THE DEFENDANT HAS NOT ESTABLISHED THAT
PLAINTIFF’S ASSIGNOR FAILED TO ATTEND IME

While it is not disputed that recovery of no-fault benefits can be denied upon a willful failure to attend a properly noticed IME, the defendant has not established the plaintiff’s assignor failure to attend the IME nor has the defendant established that the IME notices even existed.

 The denials annexed to the defendant’s cross-motion clearly indicate that the claims were denied based on alleged IME no-shows on 5/13/2009 and 5/21/2009. 

IME Notices.  There is absolutely no evidence that the IME scheduling letters were proper or that they were mailed or that they even existed.  Copies of IME notices are not in the defendant’s submission.  Defendant also doesn’t include any evidence that the IME notices were mailed or where they were mailed.  Such evidence generally comes in a form of an affidavit of one with personal knowledge of mailing such notices, but none is submitted by the defendant.

IME no-shows.  In order to maintain the defense of policy non-compliance based on failure to appear at a properly noticed IME, the defendant must submit some evidence that in fact the plaintiff’s assignor did not appear for two IMEs.  Again, such evidence generally requires an affidavit of one with personal knowledge of such no-shows.  Here, the defendant does not offer any admissible evidence of two no-shows.

Defective denial.  The denial is defective on its face. Even if, arguendo, the first properly noticed IME no-show was on 5/13/2009 as the denial suggests.  The defendant would be required to provide the plaintiff’s assignor with a notice to appear for another IME.  According to the denial the second no-show occurred just eight days later on 5/21/2009. Supposing that following the first no-show the defendant swiftly mailed out the notice for the second IME, and five days are allowed for mailing, the plaintiff’s assignor would only have a three day notice.  Such notice would be improper per se.

THE DEFENDANT HAS NOT ESTABLISHED A DEFENSE
BASED ON LACK OF MEDICAL NECESSITY

The defendant’s affirmation in support of its cross-motion goes at length on the subject of medical necessity of the underlying services.  Those arguments are clearly irrelevant here, since the defense premised on the lack of medical necessity was not preserved on the denial of claim form.  Neither is there any support for the alleged lack of medical necessity.

 

WHEREFORE, it is respectfully requested that the defendant’s motion be in all respects denied, together with such other, further and different relief this court may deem just and proper.
