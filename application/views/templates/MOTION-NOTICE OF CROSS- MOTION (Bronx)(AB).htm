II.          DEFENDANT HAS FAILED TO DEMONSTRATE ITS PROFFERED DEFENCE IS CONTAINED IN THE CONTRACT OF INSURANCE

In order to assert any violation of a policy “condition, exclusion and/or provision” contained in a contract of insurance the actual insurance contract at issue must be placed into evidence. Allstate Ins. Co. V. Ganesh, 8 Misc. 3d 922 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 2005); DiCristi v. Liberty Mutual Ins. Co., 8 Misc. 3d 1027(A) (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 2005). Incontrovertibly, it is defendants burden to place such policy in evidence, and failure to proffer such evidence in admissible form is fatal to defendant’s defense as an allegation of a policy violation- absent the actual contract of insurance – is nothing more than rank hearsay and worthless. Id; See also, American Honda Finance Corp. v. Progressive Cas.Ins. Co., 290 A.D.2d 850 (3rd Dept. 2002). Clearly, such proof in admissible form must be proffered when a defendant seeks to defend against a claim based upon, inter alia: 1) The policy at issue contains a condition that the loss not be intentionally caused or exclusion if the loss was an intentional acts; 2) the policy at issue was voided due to the insured’s alleged failure to appear for an Independent Medical Examination; or 3) the policy at issue was voided due to the insured’s alleged failure to appear for an Examination Under Oath. Indeed, absent the contract of insurance being admitted into evidence defendant’s defense may not be asserted as a matter of law.

In Allstate Ins. Co. v. Ganesh, 8 Misc. 3d 922 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 2005), defendant sought a permanent stay of arbitration again plaintiff who sought coverage for injuries sustained as a result of a motor vehicle accident. Defendant alleged coverage was vitiated because its insured violated State Farm’s policy conditions, exclusions and provisions. Specifically, defendant alleged the accident at issue was not an accident, but a staged loss. In support of its contention, defendant failed to proffer the insurance contract at issue, which may or may not have contained such policy exclusions. In rejecting defendants contention the court held:

The source of any condition that the loss not be intentionally caused or exclusion of the loss was intentional is, of course, the applicable insurance policy. Although Allstate agrees that State Farm insured Balbin’s vehicle February 11,2003, and respondent Ganesh did not challenge that fact, the parties did not stipulate to any policy provisions. Because no party introduced State Farm's policy in evidence, State Farm never proved, most fundamentally, that the policy issued to Balbin excluded intentionally caused losses or losses involving particular conduct or imposed any conditions on anyone relating to a claimed loss.

Without the insurance contract itself, any recitation of the contract’s terms through testimony or other documents in evidence is rank hearsay and contrary to the best evidence rule. (People v Joseph, 86 NY2d 565, 570 [1995]; Schozer v William Penn Life Ins. Co. of N.Y., 84 NY2d 639, 643 [1994]; NW Liquidating Corp. v. Helmsley- Spear, Inc., 248 AD2d 304, 305 [1st Dept 1998]; Schiffren v Kramer, 225 AD2d 757, 758 [2d *924 Dept 1996].) The absence of State Farm’s insurance policy thus prevents any finding that a disclaimer under that policy is valid as a very threshold matter. Id. at 923-924 (Emphasis added.)

Similarly, in Di Cristi v. Liberty Mutual Ins. Co., 8 Misc.3d 1027(A) (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 2005), plaintiff initiated a personal injury action against defendant. Plaintiff filed a motion for summary judgment, and defendant opposed alleging the vehicle that struck plaintiff was not covered under a policy exclusion. Specifically, defendant alleged it was disclaiming based upon an exclusion provision of the insurance contract pertaining to the use of a vehicle as a public or livery conveyance. In support of its contention defendant failed to proffer the original insurance contract in admissible form. The court granted plaintiff’s motion and held:

The defendant produced evidence in the form of testimony from its employees who investigated the accident report and the insurance coverage. However, the defendant did not produce as evidence, the original contract.

Without the insurance contract itself, any recitation of the contract’s terms through testimony or other documents in evidence is rank hearsay and contrary to the best evidence rule (see In the Matter of the Application for a Permanent Stay of Arbitration by Allstate Insurance Co v Ganesh, 2005 NY Slip Op. 25269 [Bronx County, 2005]; People v. Joseph, 86 NY2d 565 [1995]; Schozer v William Penn Life Ins. Co. Of NY, 84 NY2d 639 [1994]). The absence of Liberty Mutual’s insurance policy thus prevents any finding that a disclaimer under that policy is valid as a very threshold matter. As a result, there is no need not address the issue of the timeliness of defendant’s disclaimer. Id. (Emphasis added.)

In American Honda Finance Corp. v. Progressive Cas. Ins. Co., 290 A.D.2d 850 (3rd Dept. 2002), plaintiff commenced a breach of contract action against defendant. Defendant asserted the defended on the ground that the contract of insurance was vitiated as the policy at issue did not cover fraud committed by the insured. Thereafter, plaintiff moved for summary judgment and defendant annexed an affidavit from its special investigator and documentary evidence in admissible form demonstrating the existence of the specific policy exclusion at issue. The lower court granted plaintiff's motion and denied defendant’s motion. Defendant appealed. In reversing the decision of the lower court and denying both motion, the Appellate Division held:

First addressing plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment, we find ne error in Supreme Court’s implicit conclusion that it sustained its prima facie burden of establishing its entitlement to judgment as a matter of law (see, Zuckerman v City of New York, 49 NY2d 557,562). In response thereto, defendant was required to produce evidentiary proof in admissible form sufficient to require a trial of a material question of fact which its claim is premised (id.). We find that in connection with its cross motion and in opposition to plaintiff’s motion, defendant submitted sufficient admissible evidence to defeat plaintiff’s motion, which included a reference to the specific policy exclusion on which it relied, the various denial letters highlighted the basis for the denial, Hare’s affidavit detailing his work as special investigator, along with the affidavit of Richard Pacheco, an employee of NETS, which referenced the report issued by NETS. At this preliminary stage of the proceeding, a question of fact is lurking as to whether Perez committed fraud or made other misrepresentations such that the policy exclusion would preclude recovery. Id. (Emphasis added.)

In the instant case, defendant has failed to place the contract of insurance at issue in the instant matter in evidence. Undoubtedly, defendant will attempt to distinguish the cases set forth supra, on the ground that such cases involved disclaimers and not the recovery of outstanding no fault benefits. However, plaintiff respectfully submits such semantics are without merit as the true holding of these cases based upon the same type of insurance contract that would be at issue in a no-fault matter. Moreover, the issues of law and rules of evidence discussed in the cases are relevant in all cases involving a contract of insurance and an allegation that there was a violation of the policy at issue.

Finally, defendant’s contention that its insured violated a policy “condition, exclusion and/or provision” is unavailing as an allegation of such- absent the actual contract of insurance- is rank hearsay and worthless. Therefore, this Honorable Court need not consider defendant’s remaining contentions as it has failed to demonstrate its insured violated a policy provision, ab inito. Accordingly, defendant’s motion must be denied.

 

III.        DEFENDANT HAS FAILED TO DEMONSTRATE THE PRE-CLAIM IME REQUESTS AND DENIALS WERE ACTUALLY MAILED

In order to support a defense based upon assignor’s alleged failure to appear for an Independent Medical Examination (hereinafter referred to as “IME”), the burden is on defendant to demonstrate prima faci: 1) The IME requests were actually mailed; and 2) plaintiff’s assignor failed to appear for the purportedly scheduled IMEs. See generally, Stephen Fogel Pychological, P.C. v. Progressive Cas, Ins.Co.,35 A.D.2d 720 (2nd Dept. 2006); Chi Acupuncture, P.C. v. Kemper Auto and Home Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 50352 (U) (App. Term 9th and 10th Jud. Dists. 2007). However, merely annexing IME appearance letters is insufficient to meet defendant’s evidentiary burden and defendant must demonstrate such requests were timely- and actually- mailed. See generally, Contemp. Med. Diag. & treatment,P.C. v. GEICO, 6 Misc. 3d 137 (A) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2005); See also, Boai Zhong Yi Acupuncture Services, P.C v. New York Central Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 12 Misc. 3d 135 (A)(App Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2006); Clark v. Columbian Mut. Life Ins. Co., 221 A.D.2d 227 (1st Dept. 1995); Function Supply v. Progressive Ins. Co., 9 Misc.3d 1123 (A) (N.Y). City Civil Ct. 2005).

Indeed, in order to demonstrate its IME requests were actually mailed, defendant must proffer either an affidavit from an individual that personally mailed the denial of claim form or an affidavit from an individual with personal knowledge of the office practice and procedure designed to ensure that items are properly addressed and mailed. See, Mega Supply & Billing, Inc. v. AIU Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 50687(U) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists.2007); Clark v. Columbian Mut. Life Ins. Co.,221 A.D.2d 227 (1st Dept. 1995); Boai Zhong Yi Acupuncture Services, PC. v. New York Central Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 12 Misc. 3d 135 (A)(App Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2006); State v. International Fidelity Ins. Co., 181 Misc. 3d 595(N.Y. Sup Ct. 1999) Acupuncture Prima Care, P.C. v. State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 52273(U) (N.Y. Dists. Ct. 2007). Similarly the affidavits of Stephen Howard and Rose Koteras fail to demonstrate defendant timely mailed the annexed denial of claim forms.

In Mega Supply & Billing. Inc. v. AIU Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 50687(U) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2007), plaintiff initiated an action against defendant to recover outstanding no-fault benefits. Thereafter, plaintiff moved for summary judgment. In opposition to plaintiff’s motion, defendant asserted its denial was based upon plaintiff’s assignor’s failure to appear at an examination under oath. In support of its contention, defendant annexed an affidavit from its administrative assistant. The lower court found defendant’s proofs demonstrated the EUO requests were actually mailed and denied plaintiff’s motion. Plaintiff appealed. In reversing the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Term held:

Defendant submitted an affidavit from an administrative assistant who stated that the first request was forwarded to plaintiff’s assignor and that she forwarded a second request to plaintiff’s assignor. Her affidavit was insufficient because she did not state that she actually mailed the letters and since she did not set forth proof of defendant’s office practice and procedure designed to ensure that items are properly addressed and mailed, it did not give rise to a presumption of mailing (citation omitted). Id. (Emphasis added.)

Similarly, in Boai Zhong Yi Acupuncture Services, P.C. v. New York Central Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 12 Misc. 3d 135 (A) (App Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2006), plaintiff brought an action against defendant to recover outstanding no-fault benefits. Plaintiff moved for summary judgment and defendant opposed alleging it timely mailed verification requests. The lower court denied plaintiff’s motion and plaintiff appealed. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Term held:

In an affidavit annexed to defendant’s opposition papers, defendant’s claim examiner stated that “[i]t id [defendant’s] business practice to mail all verification requests to the address the applicant lists on the bill on the same day the verification requests is generated. In compliance with that policy on the day [that] each verification request was generated: 2/25/02, 4/01/02 and 3/19/02, the verification request was mailed to [plaintiff].” In addition, unlike the affidavit of defendant’s claims examiner in the case of contemp. Med. Diag. & Treatment, P.C. v Government Empls. Ins. Co. (6 Misc 3d 137[A], 2005 NY Slip Op 50254[U] [App Term, 2d & 11th Jud Dists]). Defendant’s claims examiner further stated that he had “personal knowledge of [defendant’s] office practices and policies and [is] responsible for ensuring that they are enforced.” Defendant’s opposition papers thereby sufficiently established the timely mailing of the verification requests since the affidavit of defendant’s claims examiner was from one with personal knowledge of the standard office practice used by defendant to ensure that its requests were properly addressed and ,ailed (citation omitted). Id. (Emphasis added.)

Likewise, in Clark v.Columbian Mut. Life Ins. Co., 221 A.D.2d 227 (1st Dept. 1995), plaintiff initiated an action against defendant to recover the proceeds of a life insurance policy. Defendant moved for summary judgment and alleged the policy had lapsed and that it mailed plaintiff a notice of disclaimed for failing to make a payment. Defendant proffered the deposition testimony of an individual that purportedly had personal knowledge of mailing. The insurer’s witness testified as to the defendant’s regular practices and procedures. The lower court found defendant met its burden of demonstrating a presumption of mailing and plaintiff appealed. However, defendant failed to elicit testimony that the office practice was geared toward ensuring the likelihood such notices were actually mailed and Appellate Division reversed and held:

Mr. Smith testified he had no contact with the Data Processing Department of defendant which generated the notices and had little knowledge as to how this was done. Further, prior to his preparation in the present law suit, he had never seen the computer print-out which listed the insured’s who were supposed to be sent notices. He also testified that he did not know whether anyone checked the printed notices to ensure that they corresponded to the computer print-out. Further, he said he did not know whether the envelopes he took to the post office were correctly addressed or if they contained all of the notices that were supposed to be sent that day, and he never counted the number of envelopes to ensure that they corresponded to the number of envelopes indicated on the affidavit he signed.

Although, therefore, defendant submitted testimony on the preparation and mailing of the notices, it did not include sufficient evidence to ensure that the notices mailed corresponded to and included all of the names on the computer print-out or the correct addresses of those individuals. Thus, there was no showing that anyone compared “the names on the mailing list with the names and addresses on the envelopes for accuracy, or whether anyone routinely checked that the total number of envelopes matched the number of names on the mailing list.” Id. at 228-229. (Emphasis added.)

In State v. Internationally Fidelity Ins. Co.,  181 Misc.3d 595 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1999), the court was confronted with the issue of whether the general standard of setting forth routine practices and procedures establishes a presumption of mailing when items are sent via certified mail. The court rejected the existence of such presumption as to certified mail and held:

The only New York court to examine the issue held that “ where the method of mailing requires the addressee to sign a return receipt, the presumption of receipt which attaches to first class mail is inapplicable, because the mailing cannot be received until the addressee signs for it” (Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. v. Young, 157 Misc.2d 452,454,596 N.Y.S.2d 653).

 

When an item is mailed by certified mail, return receipt requested, the Post Office’s duty would not end with the mere transmission of the letter. After transmitting the letter, the Post Office must obtain the recipient’s signature on the green receipt card and return the receipt to the sender of the certified mail. The court presumes that what ordinarily results from the transmission of, or failure to transmit, a certified mail letter probably resulted in the given case. Therefore, if the notices were sent and had been signed for, the Post Office would have delivered seven green cards to the sender, IFIC’s failure to produce the green receipt cards is fatal to their claim of “presumption of receipt”. Clearly, the distinction makes sense. The very reason for transmitting mail by certified mail is to avoid reliance on the presumption of receipt Id.

Most recently, in Acupuncture Prima Care, P.C. v. State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 52273 (U) (N.Y. Dist, Ct. 2007), the honorable judge Engel vividly set forth the requirements of the Appellate Court regarding defendant’s substantial burden in raising a presumption of mailing. To wit:

When stripped of all of its excess verbiage, which is designed to meet the aforesaid proof of mailing requirements, all that Mr. Fanfan actually alleges is that, at the Melville, New York office, an NF-10 is retrieved from a printer, placed in an envelope, brought to the mail room and picked up by a courier who delivers the envelope to the United States Postal Service. It is the opinion of this court that while this may describe a “standard office practice and procedure” Residential Holding Corp. v. Scottsdale Insurance Company,supra, it does not describe one “used to ensure that items were properly addressed and mailed.”

Absent from the Defendant’s alleged office practice and procedure is any indication that there exists a practice of comparing the names and addressed on the NF-10s with that of the Plaintiff’s billing, or the existence of a mailing list used to compare the names and addresses on the NF-10s with the items mailed, or whether a list is maintained indicating the number of NF-10s generated on a given day along with some identification of the matters in which the NF-10s were generated, or whether anyone routinely checked to see if the total number of envelopes mailed matched the number of NF-10s generated on a particular day, or a certificate of mailing identifying the items allegedly delivered to the United States Postal Service. See: Matter of Lumbermens Mut. Casualty Co., 135 AD2d 373, 521 NYS2d 432 (1st Dept. 1987); State-Wide Insurance Co. v. Simmons, 201 AD2d 655, 608 NYS2d 274 (2nd Dept.1994); Clark v. Columbian Mut. Life Insurance Co., 221 AD2d 227,633 NYS2d 311 (1st Dept. 1995); L.Z.R. Raphaely Galleries, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co., 191 AD2d 680, 595 NYS2d 802 (2nd Dept.1993)

Without safeguards, notwithstanding the fact that the Defendant follows the same procedure, there is no way for the Defendant, or the court for that matter, to determine whether or not all NF-10s generated by the Defendant on a particular day are actually mailed. The procedure Mr. Fanfan describes does not take into account the possibility that an item of mail might get misplaced or lost anywhere between the CSA pool and the United States Post Office. If this were to occur, under the practices and procedures described by Mr.Fanfan, no one would know and the Defendant would have no way of tracking the lost NF-10. Compare: Presutto v. Travelers Insurance Company, 17 Misc 3d 1121(A); 2007 NY Spli Op. 52095

In addition to these problems with the Defendant’s proof of mailing, nowhere do Mr. Fanfan or Ms. O’Tey-Simon indicate how they know the office practice and procedure Mr. Fanfan describes was followed in this case. See: Allstate Social Work and Psychological Svcs PLLC v. GEICO General Insurance Co., 6 Misc 3d 1010(A), 800 NYS2d 341 (Civ. Ct. Kings Co. 2005); S Capri Medical, P.C. v. Auto One Insurance Company, 14 Misc 3d 1205 (A), 836 NYS2d 483 (Civ. Ct. Kings Co. 2006)

Based on the discussion supra, it is clear defendant has failed to demonstrate it actually mailed the IME requests. Indeed, defendant has failed to annex an affidavit from an individual with personal knowledge of the facts. Defendant’s contentions are conclusory, not in admissible form, and therefore, worthless. The affidavit of Stephen Howard, the “Director of Operations” is conclusory and does nor demonstrate that she possesses personal knowledge that defendant possesses and “office practice and procedure designed to ensure that items are properly addressed and mailed.”

A mere cursory review of the affidavits reveals such are completely lacking and even fails to address the hearsay problem with the letters annexed to the motion. Indeed, none of the letters annexed to defendants’ motion have met the elemental requirement that such proof be placed into evidence under the business records exception to the hearsay rule. Undoubtedly, defendant is required to proffer an affidavit from an individual with personal knowledge setting forth the elements of the business records exception to the hearsay rule in order for such letters to be considered.

Generally, records made in the regular course of business are hearsay when offered for the truth of their contents. See generally, Richardson on Evidence §8-301 (Farrell, 11th Ed. 1995). Notwithstanding the general rule, a document shall be admitted into evidence under the business record exception to the hearsay rule if: 1) The document is made in the regular and ordinary course of business; 2) It is the regular and ordinary course of such business to make the document; and 3) The document is made at the time of the event or within a reasonable time thereafter. See generally, C.P.L.R.R.4518 (a).

Defendant has failed at this aspect of its prima facie case. Accordingly, defendant’s motion must be denied in its entirety. Assuming, arguendo, this Honorable Court determines defendant demonstrated the mailing of the IME requests-which it did not- defendant’s motion should still be denied as defendant has failed to meet the second prong of the test discussed supra-nonappearance.

 

IV.         DEFENDANT HAS FAILED TO DEMONSTRATE PLAINTIFF’S ASSIGNOR FAILED TO APPEAR FOR THE SCHEDULED IMEs

As previously discussed supra, it is incumbent upon defendant to demonstrate plaintiff’s assignor failed to appear for the scheduled IMEs. Indeed, defendant is required to proffer an affidavit from an individual with personal knowledge sufficient to demonstrate plaintiff’s assignor did not appear. See generally, Stephen Fogel Pychological, P.C. v. Progressive Cas, Ins. Co., 35 A.D.2d 720(2nd Dept. 20060; Chi Acupuncture, P.C. v. Kemper Auto and Home Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 50352(U) (App. Term 9th and 10th Jud. Dists. 2007). Failure to provide such proof in admissible form renders defendant’s position impotent and its motion must be denied.

In Stephen Fogel Pychological, P.C. v. Progressive Cas, Ins. Co., 35 A.D.2d 720 (2nd Dept. 2006), plaintiff initiated an action against defendant to recover outstanding first part no-fault benefits. Defendants moved to dismiss and plaintiff cross-moved for summary judgment. Defendant alleged plaintiff violated a policy condition by failing to appear for two scheduled IMEs. Plaintiff opposes, asserting defendant failed to demonstrate such notice were actually mailed and failed to show plaintiff did not appear. The lower court denied defendant’s motion and granted plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment. Defendant appealed. On appeal, the Appellate Term modified the decision of the lower court and denied plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment. The parties cross-appealed. The Appellate Division affirmed and held:

In support of its motion for summary judgment, Progressive was required to establish, prima facie, that it mailed the notices of the IMEs to Kim and that he failed to appear for the IMEs. Progressive failed to meet its burden by proof in admissible form, because it submitted no evidence from anyone with personal knowledge of the mailings or of the nonappearances. (citations omitted). Given Progressive’s failure to meet its burden, denial of its motion was required without consideration of Fogel’s opposition papers. Id. at 720 (Emphasis added.)

Similarly, in Chi Acupuncture, P.C. v. Kemper Auto and Home Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 50352(U) (App. Term 9th and 10th Jud. Dists. 2007), plaintiff initiated an action recover outstanding no-fault benefits. Defendant moved for summary judgment on the ground that plaintiff’s assignor failed to appear for two independent medical exams. The lower court found defendant demonstrated it issues a timely denial of claim form and that the IME requests were mailed. The lower court denied defendant’s motion. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Term held:

While defendant herein established proper mailing of the IME requests, it did not submit evidence in admissible form from anyone with personal knowledge of the nonappearances. Since defendant failed to meet its burden, its motion summary judgment was properly denied. Id. (Emphasis added.)

In supports of its contention that plaintiff’s assignor failed to appear defendant again erroneously relies upon the affidavit of Stephen Howard, who alleges to have personal knowledge of the non-appearance, but such is based upon rank hearsay. Clearly, such a statement cannot be considered by this Honorable Court. Since such statement is defendant’s only basis in support of its contention plaintiff’s assignor failed to appear its motion must be denied in its entirety.

IN SUPPORT OF PLAINTIFF’S CROSSMOTION

 

V.           PLAINTIFF’S PRIMA FACIE CASE: SUBMISSION OF CLAIM AND MAYMENT OVERDUE

In order to obtain first-party no-fault benefits for health care services provided to an assignor, a plaintiff establishes its prima facie entitlement to summary judgment by proof that: 1) Plaintiff submitted its claim setting forth the fact and amount of the loss; and 2) payment of such benefits is overdue. See generally, N.Y. Ins. Law §5106(a); Mary Immaculate Hospital v. Allstate Ins. Co., 5 A.D.3d 742 (2nd Dept. 2004); New York Hospital Medical Center of Queens v. MVAIC, 12 A.D.3d 429 (2nd Dept. 2004); Empire State Psychological Services, P.C v. Travelers Ins. Co., 13 Misc. 3d 131(A) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2006); Amaze Medical Supply Inc. v. Eagle Ins. Co., 2 Misc.3d 128(A) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2003); A.B. Medical Services, P.L.L.C. v. GEICO, 2Misc. 3d 26 (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists.2003); Amaze Medical Supply Ins. v. Allstate, 3 Misc.3d 43 (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2004); Jeff Mollins, D.C. v. MVAIC, 14 Misc.3d 133(A) (App. Term 1st Dept. 2007); East Coast Psychological, P.C. v. Allstate Ins. Co., 13 Misc. 3d 133(A) (App. Term 1st Dept. 2006); Response Med. Equip. v. General Assurance Co., 13 Misc.3d 129 (A) (App. Term 1st Dept. 2006).

Once plaintiff establishes its prima facie case, the burden shifts to defendant to raise a triable issue of fact or succumb. Absent any material issue of fact, summary judgment must be entered in favor of plaintiff. See generally,  C.P.L.R. R.3212; See also, Alvarez v. Prospect Hosp. Alvarez v. Prospect Hosp., 68 N.Y.2d 320 (1986); Zuckerman v. City of New York, 49 N.Y.2d 557 (1980). Plaintiff respectfully submits it has established its prima facie case and judgment is warrented.

VI.         DEFENDANT HAS ADMITTED TO PLAINTIFF’S PRIMA FACIE CASE

A formal judicial admission is an act of a party done during the course of litigation-which dispenses with the production of evidence by conceding- the truth of the facts. See generally, Richardson on Evidence §8-215 (Farrell, 11th Ed. 1995) (Emphasis added). Most importantly, a formal judicial admission is not evidence, but rather, takes the place of evidence. Id; See also, Wigmore, Evidence §1058. Therefore, formal judicial admissions are conclusive of the facts admitted. Id. Indeed, a verified answer to interrogatories constitutes a formal judicial admission against such party. See generally, Fair Price Medical Supply, Inc. v. St. Paul Travelers Insurance Company, 2007 N.Y. Slip Op. 27173 (App. Term 1st Dept. 2007); Bigelow v. Acands, Inc., 196 A.D.2d 436 (1st Dept. 1993); United Bank Limited v. Cambridge Sporting Goods Corp., 41 N.Y.2d 254 (1976).

In Fair Price Medical Supply, Inc. v. St. Paul Travelers Insurance Company, 2007 N.Y. Slip Op. 27173 (App. Term 1st Dept. 2007), plaintiff initiated an action against defendant to recover outstanding no-fault benefits. At trial, plaintiff presented no witnesses, but rather, established its prima facie case through defendant’s response to its interrogatories. Defendant objected to the use of the interrogatories as admissions. The lower court rejected defendant’s argument. Defendant failed to introduce a timely denial of claim form into evidence demonstrating a preserved defense and judgment was entered in favor of the plaintiff. Defendant appealed. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Term held:

In response to plaintiff’s interrogatories, defendant insurer admitted that it received the no-fault claims at issue and made partial payment on the claims. Inasmuch as defendant’s verified answers to the interrogatories constituted admissions of a party, which are admissible as evidence (see Bigelow v Acands, Inc., 196 AD2d 436 [1993]), defendant may not now be heard to argue that plaintiff failed to submit proof that the claims had been mailed and received, and that that were overdue(see Mary Immaculate Hosp. v Allstate Ins. Co., 5 AD3d 742 [2004]). To the extent that Empire State Psychological Servs. V Travelers Ins. Co., 13 Misc 3d 131 (A) (2006) supports a contrary conclusion, we decline to follow it. Id. (Emphasis added.)

In Bigelow v Acands,Inc., 196 A.D.2d 436 (1st Dept. 1993), plaintiff brought an action against defendant to recover damages for injuries obtained by inhaling asbestos. At trial, a jury verdict found in favor of plaintiff and found defendant one-hundred percent liable for the damages and defendant appealed. The Appellate Division modified the verdict on the issue of apportionment and held:

Since a new trial id necessary on apportionment, we note that, to the extent that plaintiffs’ answers to interrogatories listed various companies which contributed to their asbestos exposure, they constituted admissions of a party and were admissible as evidence (United Bank v Cambridge Sporting Goods Corp., 41 NY2d 254, 264). Id.(Emphasis added.)

In the instant case, defendant has admitted to plaintiff’s prima facie case, albeit in a different manner than responding to plaintiff’s interrogatories. However, applying the reasoning of the decision set forth supra, defendant’s formal judicial admissions contained in its motion established plaintiff’s prima facie case. Indeed, a motion for summary judgment and the affidavits contained therein constitute “an act of a party done during the course of litigation.” A review of the motion and the corresponding affidavits reveals that defendant admits to plaintiff’s prima facie case. Annexed to defendant’s motion as “Exhibit E” is the affidavit of Roseanne Koteras- defendant’s claims representative. Mrs. Koteras admits to receiving plaintiff’s bill and that payment was not made-rather a denial was issued (See affidavit of Roseanne Koteras annexed to defendant’s motion “Exhibit E”). Clearly, defendant has admitted to plaintiff’s case plaintiff is entitled to summary judgment.

VII.       IN THE ALTERNATIVE, PLAINTIFF REQUESTS PARTIAL SUMMARY JUDGMENT AS TO ITS PRIMA FACIE CASE

Pursuant to C.P.L.R. R.3212(e) this Honorable Court has “the authority to grant summary judgment as to part of but a single cause of action, “to wit, plaintiff’s prima facie case. Indeed, the use of “the partial summary judgment procedure should be fully utilized”. Janos v. Peck, 21 A.D.2d 529 (1st Dept.), aff’d 15 N.Y.2d 509, 254 (1964). Clearly, the Appellate Courts have found this to be a viable application of C.P.L.R. R.3212(e). See generally,  Tow v. Moore, 24 A.D.2d 648 (2nd Dept. 1965); Aronov v. Regency Garden Apartments Corp., 34 A.D.3d 404 (2nd Dept. 2006)

In Tow v. Moore, 24 A.D.2d 648 (2nd Dept. 1965), plaintiff brought an action against defendant for a dissolution of their partnership and an accounting. Defendant moved to dismiss plaintiff’s complaint. The lower court granted defendant’s motion, solely to the issue of the dissolution of corporate entities, and where such was not clear a trial was necessary to establish the status of the parties. Defendant appealed. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Division held:

On this motion to discuss the complaint, since the major portion of the action asserted therein is insufficient and since such portion is severable from the remaining good portion, summary judgment may properly be granted dismissing the severable portion which we have found to be insufficient (CPLR 5012; CPLR 3211, subd. [c]; CPLR 3212, subd. [e]; Melniker v. American Title & Guaranty Company, 253 App.Div. 570, 3 N.Y.S.2d 198; cf. Demuth v. Griffin, 253 App.Div. 399, 402-403, N.Y.S.2d 2, 4-5). Id. at 404. (Emphasis added.)

Furthermore, in Aronov v. Regency Garden Apartments Corp., 34 A.D.3d 404 (2nd Dept. 2006) plaintiff brought an action to recover damages for wrongful death and conscious pain and suffering. Defendant moved to dismiss the portion of plaintiff’s cause of action as pertaining to wrongful death. The lower court granted defendant’s motion and plaintiff appealed. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Division held:

In disposing of the motion, the order appealed from articulated the Supreme Court’s determination in sufficient detail (see CPLR 2219), and thus made clear that so much of the plaintiff’s cause of action as sought to recover damages for her decedent’s conscious pain and suffering was not dismissed. Rather, upon dismissal, that portion of the cause of action which sought to recover damages for wrongful death effectively severed from that portion of the cause of action which sought to recover damages for conscious pain and suffering. Id. at 404.

Assuming, arguendo, this Honorable Court determines defendant has preserved a viable defense in a timely denial of claim form-which it has not yet done-partial summary judgment should be entered in favor of plaintiff as to its prima facie case. Indeed, although triable issues of fact may exists as to defendant’s proposed defense, it would be unnecessary for plaintiff to proffer further support for its prima facie case, such issues must be further elucidated at trial. However, plaintiff respectfully submits it has met its burden and it is entitled to summary judgment.
