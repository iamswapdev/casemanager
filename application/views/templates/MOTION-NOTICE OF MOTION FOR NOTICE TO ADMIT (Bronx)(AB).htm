II. PLAINTIFFS PRIMA FACIE CASE: SUBMISSION OF CLAIM AND PAYMENT OVERDUE

In order to obtain first-party no-fault benefits for health care services provided to an assignor, a plaintiff establishes its prima facie entitlement to summary judgment by proof that: 1) plaintiff submitted its claim setting forth the fact and amount of the loss; and 2) payment of such benefits is overdue. See generally, N.Y. Ins. Law §5106(a); Mary Immaculate Hospital v. Allstate Ins. Co., 5 A.D,3d 742 (2nd Dept. 2004); New York Hospital Medical Center of Queens v. MVAIC, 12 A.D.3d 429 (2nd Dept. 2004); Empire State Psychological Services, P.C. v. Travelers Ins. Co., 13 Misc.3d 131(A) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2003); A.B. Medical Services, P.L.L.C. v GIECO, 2 Misc.3d 26 (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2003); Amaze Medical Supply Ins. V. Allstate, 3 Misc.3d 43 (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2004); Park Neurological Services, P.C. v.GEICO, 14 Misc.3d 95 (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2004); Jeff Mollins, D.C. v. MVAIC, 14 Misc. 3d 133(A) (App. Term 1st Dept. 2006; Response Med. Equip. v. General Assurance Co., 13 Misc.3d 129 (A) (App. Term 1st Dept. 2006).

 Once plaintiff establishes its prima facie case, the burden shifts to defendant to raise a triable issue of fact or succumb. Absent any material issue of fact, summary judgment must be entered in favor of plaintiff. See generally, C.P.L.R. R.3212; See also, Alvarez v. Prospect Hosp. Alvarez v. Prospect Hosp., 68 N.Y.2d 320 (1986); Zuckerman v. City of New York, 49 N.Y. 2d 557 (1980). Plaintiff respectfully submits it has established its prima facie case and judgment is warranted.

 

III. DEFENDANT HAS ADMITTED TO PLAINTIFFS PRIMA FACIE CASE

A formal judicial admission is an act of a party done during the course of litigation- which dispenses with the production of evidence by conceding  the truth of the facts. See generally, Richardson on Evidence §8-215 (Farrell, 11th Ed. 1995) (Emphasis added). Most importantly, a formal judicial admission is not evidence, but rather, takes the place of evidence. Id.; See also, Wigmore, Evidence §1058. Therefore, formal judicial admissions are conclusive of the facts admitted. Id. Indeed, a verified answer to interrogatories constitutes a formal judicial admission against such party. See generally, Fair Price Medical Supply, Inc. v. St. Paul Travelers Insurance Company, 2007 N.Y. Slip Op. 27173 (App. Term 1st Dept. 2007); Bigelow v. Acands, Inc., 196 A.D. 2d 436 (1st dept. 1993); United Bank Limited v. Cambridge Sporting Goods Corp., 41 N.Y. 2d 254 (1976).

 

In Fair Price Medical Supply, Inc. v. St. Paul Travelers Insurance Company, 2007 N.Y. Slip Op. 27173 (App. Term 1st Dept. 2007), plaintiff initiated an action against defendant to recover outstanding no-fault benefits. At trial, plaintiff presented no witnesses, but rather, established its prima facie case through defendants response to its interrogatories . Defendant objected to the use of the interrogatories as admissions. The lower court rejected defendants argument. Defendant failed to introduce a timely denial of claim form into evidence demonstrating a preserved defense and judgment was entered in favor of the plaintiff. Defendant appealed. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Term held:

In response to plaintiffs interrogatories, defendant insurer admitted that it received the no-fault claims at issue and made partial payment on the claims. Inasmuch as defendants of a party, which are admissible as evidence (see Bigelow v Acands, Inc., 196 AD2d 436 [1993]), defendant may not now be heard to argue that plaintiff failed to submit proof that the claims had been mailed and received, and that they were overdue (see Mary Immaculate Hosp. v. Allstate Ins. Co., 5 AD3d 742 [2004]). To the extent that Empire State Psychological Servs. V Travelers Ins. Co., 13 Misc 3d 131 (A)(2006) supports a contrary conclusion, we decline to follow it. Id. (Emphasis added.)

In Bigelow v. Acands, Inc., 196 A.D.2d 436 (1st dept. 1993), plaintiff brought an action against defendant to recover damages for injuries obtained by inhaling asbestos. At trial, a jury verdict found in favor of plaintiff and found defendant one-hundred percent liable for the damages and defendant appealed. The Appellate Division modified the verdict on the issue of apportionment and held:

Since a new trial is necessary on apportionment, we note that, to the extent that plaintiffs answers to interrogatories listed various companies which contributed admissions of a party and were admissible as evidence (United Bank v Cambridge Sporting Goods Corp., 41 NY2d 254, 264). Id. (Emphasis added.)

 

Similarly, in the instant case, defendant has admitted to plaintiffs prima facie case by its verified response to plaintiffs interrogatories (See Defendants Response to Plaintiffs interrogatories annexed hereto as EXHIBIT B). Plaintiff respectfully directs this honorable Courts attention to Plaintiffs Interrogatory 8 wherein plaintiff asks defendant: 1) Whether the bill at issue was received; and 2) If payment was made on such bill. Defendant admitted to receiving the bill at issue and directed Plaintiff to attached NF-10 denial of claim forms wherein Defendant admitted to not rendered payment for the bills at issue. Therefore, based upon the discussion supra, defendant had admitted to plaintiffs prima facie case. Accordingly, absent defendants presentation of a preserved defense judgment should be entered in favor of plaintiff.

IV. IN THE ALTERNATIVE, PLAINTIFF REQUESTSTS PARTIAL SUMMARY JUDGMENT AS TO ITS PRIMA FACIE CASE

Pursuant to C.P.L.R. R.3212(e) this Honorable Court has the authority to grant summary judgment as a part of but a single cause of action, to wit, plaintiffs prima facie case. Indeed, the use of the partial summary judgment procedure should be fully utilized. Janos v. Peck, 21 A.D.2d 529 (1st Dept.), affd 15 N.Y.2d 509,254 (1964). Clearly, the Appellate Courts have found this to be a viable application of C.P.L.R. R.3212(e). See generally, Tow v Moore, 24 A.D.2d 648 (2nd dept. 1965); Aronov v. Regency Garden Apartments Corp., 34 A.D.3d 404 (2nd Dept. 2006)

 

In Tow v Moore, 24 A.D.2d 648 (2nd Dept. 1965), plaintiff brought an action against defendant for a dissolution of their partnership and an accounting. Defendant moved to dismiss plaintiffs compliant. The lower court granted defendants motion, solely to the issue of the dissolution of corporate entities, and where such was not clear a trial was necessary to establish the status of the parties. Defendant appealed. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Division held:

On this motion to dismiss the complaint, since the major portion of the cause of action asserted therein is insufficient and since such portion is severable from the remaining good portion, summary judgment may properly be granted dismissing the severable portion which we have found to be insufficient (CPLR 5012;CPLR 3211, subd. [e]; Melniker v. American Title & Guaranty Company, 253 App. Div. 570, 3 N.Y.S.2d 198; cf. Demuth v. Griffin, 253 App. Div. 399, 402-403, 2 N.Y.S.2d 2, 4-5). Id. At 404.(Emphasis added.) Furthermore, in Aronov v. Regency Garden Apartments Corp., 34 A.D. 3d 404 (2nd Dept. 2006) plaintiff brought an action to recover damages for wrongful death and conscious pain and suffering. Defendant moved to dismiss the portion of plaintiffs cause of action as pertaining to wrongful death. The lower court granted defendants motion and plaintiff appealed. In affirming decision of the lower court, the Appellate Division held:

 

In disposing of the motion, the order appealed from articulated the Supreme Courts determination in sufficient detail (see CPLR 2219), and thus made clear that so much of the plaintiffs cause of action as sought to recover damages for her decedents conscious pain and suffering was not dismissed. Rather, upon dismissal, that portion of the cause of action which sought to recover damages for wrongful death was effectively severed from that portion of the cause of action which sought to recover damages for conscious pain and suffering. Id.  At 404.

 

Assuming, arguendo, this Honorable Court determines defendant has preserved a viable defense in a timely denial of claim form-which it has not yet done-partial summary judgment should be entered in favor of plaintiff as to its prima facie case. Indeed, although triable issues of fact may exist as to defendants proposed defense, it would be unnecessary for plaintiff to proffer further support for its prima facie case, such issues must be further elucidated at trial. However, plaintiff respectfully submits it has met its burden and it is entitled to summary judgment.
