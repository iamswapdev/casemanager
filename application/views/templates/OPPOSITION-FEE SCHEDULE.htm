Alek Beynenson, an attorney duly admitted to practice law before the Courts of the State of New York; hereby affirms the following to be true under the penalties of perjury:

I am a member of the law firm of The Beynenson Law Firm PC, attorneys of record for Plaintiff, and as such am fully, familiar with the facts and circumstances surrounding this action from the records and files maintained therein.

This affirmation is submitted in opposition to Defendant's motion for summary judgment dismissing plaintiff’s complaint.

 

1.            It is well-settled that on a motion for summary judgment, the moving party has the initial burden of demonstrating, by admissible evidence, its right to judgment. The burden then shifts to the opposing party, who must proffer evidence in admissible form establishing that an issue of fact exists warranting a trial (Zuckerman v City of New York, 49 NY2d 557, 562; GTF Mktg. v Colonial Aluminum Sales, 66 NY2d 965,967-968; Singer v Friedman, 220 AD2d 574, 577; Frost v County of Rensselaer, 220 AD2d 969,970) For the reasons outlined below, the defendant’s motion should be denied in its entirety.

DEFENDANT HAS FAILED TO PROFFER AN AFFIDAVIT FROM A FEE SCHEDULE EXPERT

2.      Generally, the applicability, use and interpretation of a fee schedule must be supported by an affidavit from an expert in the analysis of applicable fee schedule. See generally, Robert Half Personnel Agencies, Inc. v. Certified Management Corp., 102 Misc2d 317 (App. Term 1st Dept 1979). Failing to proffer such an affidavit, accompanied by the actual fee schedule being relied upon must result in the denial of defendant’s motion for summary judgment. Id.

3.      In Robert Half Personnel Agencies, Inc. v. Certified Management Corp., 102 Misc.2d 317 (App. Term 1st Dept. 1979), plaintiff brought an action against defendant to recover certain fees. After trial, the lower court ruled in favor of plaintiff and defendant appealed. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Term held:

The fair and reasonable value of plaintiff’s services was established by the plaintiff’s witness who qualified as an expert on employment agency fees, a status not disputed by defendant.

He testified that on a salary of $22,000, the “standard fee would be 22%”. In Pilot Employment Serv. V Afrointernational Corp. (supra), this court considered an employment agency contract wherein the oral agreement of the parties was silent as to the amount of the agency’s fee. We there adopted the fee schedule contained in subdivisions 4 and 7 of section 185 of the General Business Law “In the absence of any other testimony”. In this case, since the testimony as to the value of the services rendered was supplied by an expert witness, recourse to the schedule set forth in subdivision 7 of section 185 of the General Business Law would not be required. Id. at 318 (Emphasis added.)

 

4.      In Murali v. Upton, 175 Misc.2d 186 (N.Y. City. Civ. Ct 1997), a health care provider sued a no-fault patient to recover a fee after an insurer failed to make payment. The defendant moved for summary judgment in the ground that the fee exceeded the authorized charge. In support of its motion defendant annexed an attorney affirmation and various sections of the Worker’s Compensation Fee Schedule, which purportedly demonstrated the procedure was billed beyond the maximum allowable charge. In denying defendant’s motion the court held:

Here, even assuming, arguendo, the applicability of defense counsel’s analysis, defendants apparently still owe money to plaintiff toward the amount that defendants claim is the legally permissible maximum fee. Defendants’ analysis, however, was not supported by an affidavit equivalent to expert witness testimony, as to the applicability, use and interpretation of the annexed schedules and the components of defendants’ calculation the alleged maximum permissible fee. Id. (Emphasis added.)

 

5.      Similarly, in the instant case, defendant contends plaintiff has billed beyond the legally permissible fee set forth in the Worker’s Compensation Fee Schedule and its not entitled to its requested relief. Plaintiff respectfully submits defendant has failed to support its defense with the required proofs including, inter alia: 1) an affidavit from an individual that can be qualified as an expert in the interpretation and analysis for the Worker’s Compensation Fee Schedule. Indeed, in the absence of such proofs defendant’s motion must be denied in its entirety.

 

6.      Furthermore, Defendant is alleging that it paid two similar claims and attaches to copied of cancelled checks in the amount of $411.77 and 246.40 which checks have absolutely no explanation of benefits on it for the dates of service at issue. Some unidentified person scribbled the notations: “2-2-10 to 2-12-10” and ‘1-18-10 to 1-22-10” without explanation on the bottom of the checks. It is not clear from Defendant’s “proofs” what services were allegedly paid and how they are concurrent under the fee schedule.

 

7.      Defendant alleges that the dates of service at issue were duplicative of the dates of dates of service that have already been paid to other providers for similar modalities. Defendant’s burden is to prove this with proof in admissible form. The cancelled checks defendant attached also have no explanation of benefits. It is impossible to decipher, based upon Defendant’s submitted proofs if other similar services were paid on the same dates of service at issue. In any event Defendant would need an explanation of benefits and an affidavit of a fee schedule expert to prove this. In the case at bar, Defendant attaches no such proofs

 

8.      Defendant denied the balance of the claims based on a so called IME done by its Dr. However this defense is only preserved if defendant’s report is in admissible form and said defense is raised in a proper and timely denial of claim form.

 

 

THE BUSINESS RECORD EXCEPTION TO THE HEARSAY RULE AND MEDICAL REPORTS

9.      Generally, any business record may be admitted into evidence pursuant to C.P.L.R 4518 if an individual familiar with the practices and procedures of the business can demonstrate: 1) the document is made in the regular and ordinary course of business; 2) it is the regular and ordinary course of such business to make the document; and 3) the document was created at the time of the event, or within a reasonable time thereafter. See generally, C.P.L.R 4518; See also, Richardson on Evidence 8-303 (Farrell. 11th Ed. 1995).

 

10.  However, the Appellate Division has added additional elements for records kept and maintained by medical professionals. Indeed, medical reports kept and maintained in the regular and ordinary course of business and satisfy the foundational elements of C.P.L.R 4518 will nevertheless be deemed inadmissible unless: 1) The medical report being offered into evidence is part of the systematic day-to-day business entries of a medical professional; and 2) the report is germane to the treatment and diagnosis of a patient. See generally, Wilson v. Bodian, 130 A.D.2d 221 (2nd Dept.1987); Hefte v. Bellin, 137 A.D.2d 406 (2nd Dept.1988); Komar v. Showers, 227 A.D.2d 135 (1st Dept. 1996): Progressive Northeastern Ins. Co.v. Randazzo, 24 A.D.3d 560 (2ND Dept. 2005); Bronstein- Becher v. Becher, 25 A.D.3d 796 (2ND Dept. 2006). Moreover Document that contain a doctor’s opinion or expert proof are not admissible as business records. See, Bronsteing –Becher v. Becher, 25 A.D.3d 796 (2nd Dept.2006).

 

11.  As Defendant has failed to meet these basic foundational requirements, defendant’s motion for summary judgment with respect lack of medical necessity based on a peer review must be denied in its entirety.

 

12.  Furthermore, Defendant has failed to demonstrate its denial of claim form and verification requests were timely mailed. Defendant has failed to proffer either an affidavit from an individual that personally mailed the denial of claim form and verification requests of an affidavit from an individual with personal knowledge of the office practice and procedure designed to ensure that items are properly addressed and mailed. See, Mega Supply & Billing, Inc v. AIU Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. OP.50687 (U) (App.Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2007); Clark v. Clumbian Mut. Life Ins. Co., 221 A.D.2d 227 (1st Dept. 1995); Boai Zhong Yi Acupuncture Services, P.C. v. New York Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 12 Misc. 3d 135 (A) (App Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists.2006); State v. International Fidelity Ins. Co., 181 Misc.3d 595 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1999); Acupuncture Prima Care, P.C. v State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 52273 (U) (N.Y. Dist. Ct. 2007)

 

13.   As defendant’s mailing affidavits are conclusory and without proper foundation, Defendant’s peer review cannot be considered by this Court.

WHEREFORE, plaintiff respectfully requests this Honorable Court issue an order denying defendant’s motion and for any other relief Honorable Court deems just and proper.
