AFFIRMATION IN OPPOSITION TO DEFENDANT’S MOTION

 

 

Alek Beynenson, an attorney duly admitted to practice law before the Courts of the State of New York; hereby affirms the following to be true under the penalties of perjury:

I am a member of The Beynenson Law Firm P.C., attorneys of record for Plaintiff, and as such am fully, familiar with the facts and circumstances surrounding this action from the records and files maintained therein.

This affirmation is submitted in opposition to defendant's motion for Summary Judgment based on purported failure to appear for Independent Medical Examination (“IME”), and in support of plaintiff’s cross motion for Summary Judgment based on defendant’s invalid IME scheduling letters.

THE DEFENDANT HAS NOT ESTABLISHED THAT PLAINTIFF’S
ASSIGNOR WILLFULLY MISSED THE APPONTMENT

The defendant’s refusal to pay the plaintiff’s claim is based upon the allegation that the plaintiff’s assignor failed to attend an IME and, therefore, breached a policy condition. The defendant, however, has failed to establish that there was a material breach of the policy. A claimant’s mere failure to attend and IME is not sufficient to establish that the policy was breached.

While it is not disputed that recovery of no-fault benefits can be denied upon a willful failure to attend an IME, the defendant has not established the plaintiff’s assignor failure to attend the IME was a willful failure. As recently held by the Supreme Court, New York County. In order to establish willful failure constituting a material breach of the policy, plaintiff must show that the defendant’s attitude ‘was one of willful and avowed obstruction.’ Involving a ‘pattern of noncooperation for which no reasonable excuse is offered’. Unitrin Advantage Inc. Co. v. Andrew Carothers, M.D., 17 Misc. 1121(A) (Supreme Court, New York County, August 20, 2007) quoting Ingarra v. General Acc./PG Ins. Co. of NY, 273  A.D. 2d 766 (3rd Dept. 2003). “The insurer’s burden of proving willfulness is a heavy one.” Id.

At bar, the defendant has not come close to proving this “high burden” that the plaintiff’s assignor’s failure to appear for the IME was willful. The defendant merely asserts that the plaintiff’s assignor failed to attend the IME, after IME request letters were mailed to him. The mere fact that the plaintiff’s assignors failed to submit to the IME , after allegedly being notified about the IME  via letter in no way demonstrates a willful failure to attend the IME. As there was no willful failure to attend the IME, there was no policy breach and any denial based on that assertion is invalid as a matter of law.

Furthermore, the defendant has failed to attach a copy of the policy to its motion. As such, the defendant has not established that the failure to appear for an IME is a policy violation. According to the defendant’s denial, the claim was denied “due to non-compliance of policy conditions.” Without a copy of the policy, the defendant cannot establish that the alleged non-appearance was even a policy violation. Accordingly, the defendant has not met its burden for summary judgment.

 

THE IME SCHEDULING LETTERS WERE DEFECTIVE

 

The Regulations clearly put the expense of attending an IME or EUO on the insurance company, not the claimant. 11 NYCRR § 65-3.5. Under the statute, every scheduling letter must notify the claimant that the claimant will be reimbursed for any lost earnings and reasonable transportation expenses.  Importantly, failure to include such notice renders the scheduling letters defective even if the claimant is unemployed and does not require transportation.

Here, both IME scheduling letters included the following language: “Please make the necessary arrangements to bring an interpreter if required.”  Exhibit E of defendant’s moving papers. This clause improperly imposes the cost and the logistics of engaging an interpreter for an IME or EUO on the claimant.  As with the lost earnings and reasonable transportation expenses, it is irrelevant whether the claimant actually required an interpreter.  The defendant’s attempt to shift of the expense of an interpreter from itself onto the claimant renders the scheduling letters defective and the denial based upon them cannot be sustained.  See Milan Medical P.C. v. Kemper/Lumbermans Mutual Ins. Co., American Arbitration Association Case No. 412007044620 (2008). Exhibit A.

 

WHEREFORE,  plaintiff respectfully requests this Honorable Court issue an order denying defendant’s motion and  granting the plaintiff Summary Judgment in the amount of $878.67, for the outstanding medical bills, together with the statutory interest statutory attorneys fees and the cost and disbursement of this action, together with such further relief as this Court may deem just and proper.
