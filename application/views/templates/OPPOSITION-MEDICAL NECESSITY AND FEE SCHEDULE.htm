AFFIRMATION IN OPPOSITION TO DEFENDANT’S MOTION

 

Return Date :

 

 

Alek Beynenson, an attorney duly admitted to practice law before the Courts of the State of New York; hereby affirms the following to be true under the penalties of perjury:

I am a member of the law firm of The Beynenson Law Firm PC, attorneys of record for Plaintiff, and as such am fully, familiar with the facts and circumstances surrounding this action from the records and files maintained therein.

This affirmation is submitted in opposition to Defendant's motion for summary judgment dismissing plaintiff’s complaint.

 

1.            It is well-settled that on a motion for summary judgment, the moving party has the initial burden of demonstrating, by admissible evidence, its right to judgment. The burden then shifts to the opposing party, who must proffer evidence in admissible form establishing that an issue of fact exists warranting a trial (Zuckerman v City of New York, 49 NY2d 557, 562; GTF Mktg. v Colonial Aluminum Sales, 66 NY2d 965,967-968; Singer v Friedman, 220 AD2d 574, 577; Frost v County of Rensselaer, 220 AD2d 969,970) For the reasons outlined below, the defendant’s motion should be denied in its entirety.

 

DEFENDANT HAS FAILED TO PROFFER AN AFFIDAVIT

FROM A FEE SCHEDULE EXPERT

2.      Generally, the applicability, use and interpretation of a fee schedule must be supported by an affidavit from an expert in the analysis of applicable fee schedule. See generally, Robert Half Personnel Agencies, Inc. v. Certified Management Corp., 102 Misc2d 317 (App. Term 1st Dept 1979). Failing to proffer such an affidavit, accompanied by the actual fee schedule being relied upon must result in the denial of defendant’s motion for summary judgment. Id.

3.      In Robert Half Personnel Agencies, Inc. v. Certified Management Corp., 102 Misc.2d 317 (App. Term 1st Dept. 1979), plaintiff brought an action against defendant to recover certain fees. After trial, the lower court ruled in favor of plaintiff and defendant appealed. In affirming the decision of the lower court, the Appellate Term held:

The fair and reasonable value of plaintiff’s services was established by the plaintiff’s witness who qualified as an expert on employment agency fees, a status not disputed by defendant. He testified that on a salary of $22,000, the “standard fee would be 22%”. In Pilot Employment Serv. V Afrointernational Corp. (supra), this court considered an employment agency contract wherein the oral agreement of the parties was silent as to the amount of the agency’s fee. We there adopted the fee schedule contained in subdivisions 4 and 7 of section 185 of the General Business Law “In the absence of any other testimony”. In this case, since the testimony as to the value of the services rendered was supplied by an expert witness, recourse to the schedule set forth in subdivision 7 of section 185 of the General Business Law would not be required. Id. at 318 (Emphasis added.)

4.      In Murali v. Upton, 175 Misc.2d 186 (N.Y. City. Civ. Ct 1997), a health care provider sued a no-fault patient to recover a fee after an insurer failed to make payment. The defendant moved for summary judgment in the ground that the fee exceeded the authorized charge. In support of its motion defendant annexed an attorney affirmation and various sections of the Worker’s Compensation Fee Schedule, which purportedly demonstrated the procedure was billed beyond the maximum allowable charge. In denying defendant’s motion the court held:

Here, even assuming, arguendo, the applicability of defense counsel’s analysis, defendants apparently still owe money to plaintiff toward the amount that defendants claim is the legally permissible maximum fee. Defendants’ analysis, however, was not supported by an affidavit equivalent to expert witness testimony, as to the applicability, use and interpretation of the annexed schedules and the components of defendants’ calculation the alleged maximum permissible fee. Id. (Emphasis added.)

5.      Similarly, in the instant case, defendant contends plaintiff has billed beyond the legally permissible fee set forth in the Worker’s Compensation Fee Schedule and its not entitled to its requested relief. Plaintiff respectfully submits defendant has failed to support its defense with the required proofs including, inter alia: 1) an affidavit from an individual that can be qualified as an expert in the interpretation and analysis for the Worker’s Compensation Fee Schedule. Indeed, in the absence of such proofs defendant’s motion must be denied in its entirety.

THE BUSINESS RECORD EXCEPTION TO THE HEARSAY RULE

AND MEDICAL REPORTS

6.      Generally, any business record may be admitted into evidence pursuant to C.P.L.R 4518 if an individual familiar with the practices and procedures of the business can demonstrate: 1) the document is made in the regular and ordinary course of business; 2) it is the regular and ordinary course of such business to make the document; and 3) the document was created at the time of the event, or within a reasonable time thereafter. See generally, C.P.L.R 4518; See also, Richardson on Evidence 8-303 (Farrell. 11th Ed. 1995).

7.      However, the Appellate Division has added additional elements for records kept and maintained by medical professionals. Indeed, medical reports kept and maintained in the regular and ordinary course of business and satisfy the foundational elements of C.P.L.R 4518 will nevertheless be deemed inadmissible unless: 1) The medical report being offered into evidence is part of the systematic day-to-day business entries of a medical professional; and 2) the report is germane to the treatment and diagnosis of a patient. See generally, Wilson v. Bodian, 130 A.D.2d 221 (2nd Dept.1987); Hefte v. Bellin, 137 A.D.2d 406 (2nd Dept.1988); Komar v. Showers, 227 A.D.2d 135 (1st Dept. 1996): Progressive Northeastern Ins. Co.v. Randazzo, 24 A.D.3d 560 (2ND Dept. 2005); Bronstein- Becher v. Becher, 25 A.D.3d 796 (2ND Dept. 2006). Moreover Document that contain a doctor’s opinion or expert proof are not admissible as business records. See, Bronstein- Becher.

8.      As Defendant has failed to meet these basic foundational requirements, defendant’s motion for summary judgment with respect lack of medical necessity based on a peer review must be denied in its entirety.

9.      Furthermore, Defendant has failed to demonstrate its denial of claim form and verification requests were timely mailed. Defendant has failed to proffer either an affidavit from an individual that personally mailed the denial of claim form and verification requests of an affidavit from an individual with personal knowledge of the office practice and procedure designed to ensure that items are properly addressed and mailed. See, Mega Supply & Billing, Inc v. AIU Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. OP.50687 (U) (App.Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2007); Clark v. Clumbian Mut. Life Ins. Co., 221 A.D.2d 227 (1st Dept. 1995); Boai Zhong Yi Acupuncture Services, P.C. v. New York Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 12 Misc. 3d 135 (A) (App Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists.2006); State v. International Fidelity Ins. Co., 181 Misc.3d 595 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1999); Acupuncture Prima Care, P.C. v State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 52273 (U) (N.Y. Dist. Ct. 2007)

10.   As defendant’s mailing affidavits are conclusory and without proper foundation, Defendant’s peer review cannot be considered by this Court.

The Defendant’s Proof Fails to Prima Facie Demonstrate a Lack of Medical Necessity for the Treatment in Question

11.  With regard to the bill in the amount of $438.10, for dates of service 8/27/2010 through 9/17/2010, the defendant relies upon expert medical opinion to allege that the services at issue were not medically necessary. However, the defendant’s proof fails to prima facie demonstrate a lack of medical necessity for the treatment in question.

12.  To find treatment or services are not medically necessary, it must be reasonably shown by medical evidence, in consideration of the patient’s condition, circumstances, and best interest of the patient, that the treatment or services would be ineffective or that the insurer’s preferred health care treatment or lack of treatment would lead to an equally good outcome. Acupuncture Prima Care, P.C. v. State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co., 12830/07 (Civ. Ct. Nassau Co. 2007) (Emphasis Added).

13.  To meet its burden, at a minimum, the defendant must establish a factual basis and medical rationale for its asserted lack of medical necessity, Nir v. Allstate Ins. Co., 7 Misc.3d 544, 796 N.Y.S.2d 857 (Civ. Ct. Kings Co. 2005) (Emphasis Added), which is supported by evidence of the generally accepted medical/professional practices, A.B. Medical Services, P.L.L.C. v. New York Central Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 7 Misc.3d 1018(A), 801 N.Y.S.2d 229 (Civ.Ct. Kings Co. 2005) (Emphasis Added), and that the services rendered were inconsistent with those accepted practices. A.R. Medical Art,. P.C. v. State Farm Mutual Auto. Ins, Co., 11 Misc.3d 1057 (A), 815 N.Y.S.2d 493 (Civ. Ct. Kings Co. 2006)(Emphasis Added).

14.  However, conclusory opinion, standing alone, is insufficient to demonstrate the lack of medical necessity. City Wide Social Work & Psy. Serv, P.L.L.C. v. Travelers Indemnity Co., 3 Mise.3d 608, 77 N.Y.S.2d 241 (Civ. Ct. Kings Co. 2004); Ultimate Medical Supplies v. Lancer Ins. Co., 7 Mise.3d 1002 (A), 801 N.Y.S.2d 243 (Civ. Ct. Kings Co. 2004).

15.  In the case at bar, the defendant’s expert has failed to explain how the treatment or services provided were ineffective. Also, given the patient’s condition, circumstances, and best interests, the expert’s opinion also falls to elaborate how not providing treatment to the patient would have lead to a better or an equally good result. The defendant’s expert opinion is conclusory and insufficient. Therefore, defendant’s proof fails to prima facie demonstrate a lack of medical necessity for the treatment in question.

16.  Moreover, CPLR 3212 (f) states: “Should it appear from affidavits submitted in opposition to the motion that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot then be stated, the court may deny the motion...” In its Motion for Summary Judgment, defendant argues that the bills at issue in this action were denied based on lack of medical necessity. In support of its argument, defendant annexes an IME report. However, the report is not in a proper form and is not sufficient to support a motion. (As the burden of coming forward with admissible evidence is stronger for a movant than a respondent).

17.  Further, in the above IME report, the physician discusses the medical records that were being reviewed along with the bills. Such documents were not provided by plaintiff and are hearsay.

18.  Plaintiff is not in the possession of all of the information and documents that were being relied on by the peer-reviewing physician and has no ability to review or “cross-examine” such documents. Since Plaintiff lacks documents that are “essential to justify opposition”, defendant’s motion should be denied.

19.  Moreover, in AAA Chiropractic P.C. v. American Transit Ins. Co., 238 N.Y,L.J. 39 (Civ Ct Kings 2007), the court, when faced with a defendant’s cross-motion based on an IME, held,

“Once plaintiff establishes a prima facie case, which defendant has not disputed, the presentation of evidence of lack of medical necessity creates a question of fact which, if material, would be a basis to deny summary judgment to either party. [Citations omitted] Defendant’s presentation of evidence of lack of medical necessity does not, therefore, entitle it to summary judgment...” (Emphasis added)

20.  Therefore, defendant’s analysis is incorrect, and its IME report, if material, would only create a question of fact, which would not entitle the defendant to summary judgment.

Plaintiff rebuts the IME Report

21.  In the present case, Plaintiff, performed a follow-up examination on 9/7/2010. Exhibit A. That examination revealed positive findings and establishes a further treatment plan based on those findings. 

22.  The IME performed by the defendant’s physician and the follow up examination performed by the treating provider are inconsistent and in some respects contrary and create a material issue of fact which would defeat the motion.

WHEREFORE, plaintiff respectfully requests this Honorable Court issue an order denying defendant’s motion and for any other relief Honorable Court deems just and proper.
