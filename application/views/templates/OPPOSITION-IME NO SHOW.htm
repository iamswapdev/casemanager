I. DEFENDANT HAS FAILED TO DEMONSTRATE IT TIMELY MAILED ITS

VERIFICATION REQUESTS

3.       Pursuant to the no-fault insurance regulations an insurer may toll the thirty (30) days it has to pay or deny a claim, if it properly requests additional verification. An insurer is required to mail its initial verification request within fifteen days after it receives a bill. See generally, 11 NYCRR 65-3.5(b). Thereafter, if an insurer does not receive the requested verification within thirty days after the initial request - a follow up request shall be sent within ten days. See generally, 11 NYCRR 65-3.5(b).

 

4.      However, facially timely verification requests are insufficient and defendant must demonstrate its verification requests were timely mailed pursuant to the no-fault insurance regulations noted in the paragraph above. See generally, Contemp.Med. Diag. & Treatment, P.C. v. GEICO, 6 Misc. 3d 137(A) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2005); Mega Supply & Billing, Inc. v. AIU Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op.50687(U) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2007); See also, Boai Zhong Yi Acupuncture Services, P.C. v. New York Central Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 12 Misc. 3d 135 (A)(App Term 2nd and 11 th Jud. Dists. 2006); Clarkv. Columbian Mut. Life Ins. Co., 221 A.D.2d 227 (1st Dept. 1995); Function Supply v. Progressive Ins. Co., 9 Misc.3d 1123(A)(N.Y. City Civil Ct. 2005); State v. International Fidelity Ins. Co., 181 Misc.3d 595 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1999). Failure to proffer a timely verification request results in the preclusion of most defenses, including defendant's defense herein.

 

5.      Indeed, in order to demonstrate its verification requests were timely mailed, defendant must proffer either an affidavit from an individual that personally mailed the verification requests or an affidavit from an individual with personal knowledge of the office practice and procedure designed to ensure that items are properly addressed and mailed. See, Mega Supply & Billing, Inc. v. AIU Ins. Co.,2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 50687(U) (App. Term 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2007); Clark v. Columbian Mut. Life Ins. Co., 221 A.D.2d 227 (1st Dept. 1995); Boai Zhong Yi Acupuncture Services, P.C. v. New York Central Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 12 Misc. 3d 135 (A)(App Term 2nd and 11 th Jud. Dists. 2006); State v. International Fidelity Ins. Co., 181 Misc.3d 595 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1999); Acupuncture Prima Care, P.e. v. State FaI1l1 Mut. Auto Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 52273(U) (N.Y. Dist. Ct. 2007).

 

6.      In Mega Supply & Billing, Inc. v. AID Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op.50687(U) (App. TeI1l1 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2007), plaintiff initiated an action against defendant to recover outstanding no-fault benefits. Thereafter, plaintiff moved for summary judgment. In opposition to plaintiff s motion, defendant asserted its denial was based upon plaintiffs assignor's failure to appear at an examination under oath. In support of its contention, defendant annexed an affidavit from its administrative assistant. The lower court found defendant's proofs demonstrated the IME requests were actually mailed and denied plaintiff s motion. Plaintiff appealed. In reversing the decision of the lower court, the Appellate T eI1l1 held:

 

Defendant submitted an affidavit from an administrative assistant who stated that the first request was forwarded to plaintiff s assignor and that she forwarded a second request to plaintiff’s assignor. Her affidavit was insufficient because she did not state that she actually mailed the letters and since she did not set forth proof of defendant's office practice and procedure designed to ensure that items are properly addressed and mailed, it did not give rise to a presumption of mailing (citation omitted). Id. (Emphasis added.)

 

7.      Similarly, in Boai Zhong Yi Acupuncture Services, P.C. v. New York Central Mutual Fire Ins. Co., 12 Misc. 3d 135 (A)(App TeI1l1 2nd and 11th Jud. Dists. 2006), plaintiff brought an action against defendant to recover outstanding no-fault benefits. Plaintiff moved for summary judgment and defendant opposed alleging it timely mailed verification requests. The lower court denied plaintiff s motion and plaintiff appealed. In affiI1l1ing the decision of the lower court, the Appellate TeI1l1 held:

In an affidavit annexed to defendant's opposition papers, defendant's claims examiner stated that "[i]t is [defendant's] business practice to mail all verification requests to the address the applicant lists on the bill on the same day the verification request is generated. In compliance with that policy on the day [that] each verification request was generated:2/25/02, 4/01102 and 3/19/02, the verification request was mailed to [plaintiff1." In addition, unlike the affidavit of defendant's claims examiner in the case of Con temp. Med. Diag. & Treatment, P.e. v Government Empls. Ins. Co. (6 Misc 3d 137 [A], 2005 NY Slip Op S02S4[U] [App Term, 2d & 11th Jud Dists]), defendant's claims examiner further stated that he had "personal knowledge of [defendant's] office practices and policies and [is] responsible for ensuring that they are enforced. " Defendant's opposition papers thereby sufficiently established the timely mailing of the verification requests since the affidavit of defendant's claims examiner was from one with personal knowledge of the standard office practice used by defendant to ensure that its requests were properly addressed and mailed (citation omitted). Id. (Emphasis added.)

 

8.      Likewise, in Clark v. Columbian Mut. Life Ins. Co., 221 A.D.2d 227 (lst Dept. 1995), plaintiff initiated an action against defendant to recover the proceeds of a life insurance policy. Defendant moved for summary judgment and alleged the policy had lapsed and that it mailed plaintiff a notice of disclaimed for failing to make a payment. Defendant proffered the deposition testimony of an individual that purportedly had personal knowledge of mailing. The insurer's witness testified as to the defendant's regular practices and procedures. The lower court found defendant met its burden of demonstrating a presumption of mailing and plaintiff appealed. However, defendant failed to elicit testimony that the office practice was geared toward ensuring the likelihood such notices were actually mailed and the Appellate Division reversed and held:

 

Mr. Smith testified he had no contact with the Data Processing Department of defendant which generated the notices and had little knowledge as to how this was done.

Further, prior to his preparation in the present law suit, he had never seen the computer print-out which listed the insured’s who were supposed to be sent notices. He also testified that he did not know whether anyone checked the printed notices to ensure that they corresponded to the computer print-out. Further, he said that he did not know whether the envelopes he took to the post office were correctly addressed or if they contained all of the notices that were supposed to be sent that day, and he never counted the number of envelopes to ensure that they corresponded to the number of envelopes indicated on the affidavit he signed. Although, therefore, defendant submitted testimony on the preparation and mailing of the notices, it did not include sufficient evidence to ensure that the notices mailed corresponded to and included all of the names on the computer print-out or the correct addresses of those individuals. Thus, there was no showing that anyone compared "the names on the mailing list with the names and addresses on the envelopes for accuracy, or whether anyone routinely checked that the total number of envelopes matched the number of names on the mailing list." Id. at 228-229. (Emphasis added.)

 

9.      In State v. International Fidelity Ins. Co., 181 Misc.3d 595 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1999), the court was confronted with the issue of whether the general standard of setting forth routine practices and procedures establishes a presumption of mailing when items are sent via certified mail. The court rejected the existence of such presumption as to certified mail and held:

 

The only New York court to examine the issue held that "where the method of mailing requires the addressee to sign a return receipt, the presumption of receipt which attaches to first class mail is inapplicable, because the mailing cannot be received until the addressee signs for it" (Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. v. Young, 157 Misc.2d 452, 454,596 N.Y.S.2d 653).

 

* * *

When an item is mailed by certified mail, return receipt requested, the Post Office's duty would not end with the mere transmission of the letter. After transmitting the letter, the Post Office must obtain the recipient's signature on the green receipt card and return the receipt to the sender of the certified maiL The Court presumes that what ordinarily results from the transmission of, or failure to transmit, a certified mail letter probably resulted in the given case. Therefore, if the notices were sent and had been signed for, the Post Office would have delivered seven green receipt cards to the sender, IFIC. IFIC's failure to produce the green receipt cards is fatal to their claim of "presumption of receipt". Clearly, the distinction makes sense. The very reason for transmitting mail by certified mail is to avoid reliance on the presumption of receipt. Id.

 

10.   Most recently, in Acupuncture Prima Care, P.C. v. State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co., 2007 N.Y. Slip. Op. 52273(U) (N.Y. Dist. Ct. 2007), the Honorable Judge Engel vividly set forth the requirements of the Appellate Court regarding defendant's substantial burden in raising a presumption of mailing. To wit:

 

When stripped of all of its excess verbiage, which is designed to meet the aforesaid proof of mailing requirements, all that Mr. Fanfan actually alleges is that, at the Melville, New York office, an NF-I0 is retrieved from a printer, placed in an envelope, brought to the mail room and picked up by a courier who delivers the envelope to the United States Postal Service. It is the opinion of this court that while this may describe a "standard office practice and procedure" Residential Holding Corp. v. Scottsdale Insurance Company, supra, it does not describe one "used to ensure that items were properly addressed and mailed."Absent from the Defendant's alleged office practice and procedure is any indication that there exists a practice of comparing the names and addresses on the NF -lOs with that of the Plaintiffs billing, or the existence of a mailing list used to compare the names and addresses on the NF-lOs with the items mailed, or whether a list is maintained indicating the number ofNF-10s generated on a given day along with some identification of the matters in which the NF -lOs were generated, or whether anyone routinely checked to see if the total number of envelopes mailed matched the number of NF-10s generated on a particular day, or a certificate of mailing identifying the items allegedly delivered to the United States Postal Service. See: Matter of Lumbermens Mut. Casualty Co., 135 AD2d 373, 521 NYS2d 432 (1st Dept.1987); State-Wide Insurance Co. v. Simmons, 201 AD2d 655, 608 NYS2d 274 (2nd Dept.1994); Clark v. Columbian Mut. Life Insurance Co.,221 AD2d 227, 633 NYS2d 311 (1st Dept.1995); L.Z.R. Raphaely Galleries, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co., 191 AD2d 680,595 NYS2d 802 (2nd Dept. 1993) Without such safeguards, notwithstanding the fact that the Defendant follows the same procedure, there is no way for the Defendant, or the court for that matter, to determine whether or not all NF -lOs generated by the Defendant on a particular day are actually mailed. The procedure Mr. Fanfan describes does not take into account the possibility that an item of mail might get misplaced or lost anywhere between the CSA pool and the United States Post Office. If this were to occur, under the practices and procedures described by Mr. Fanfan, no one would know and the Defendant would have no way of tracking the lost NF-10. Compare: Presutto v. Travelers Insurance Company, 17 Misc 3d 1121(A); 2007 NY Slip Op. 52095 In addition to these problems with the Defendant's proof of mailing, nowhere do Mr. Fanfan or Ms. O'Tey-Simon indicate how they know the office practice and procedure Mr. Fanfan describes was followed in this case. See: Allstate Social Work and Psychological Svcs PLLC v. GEICO General Insurance Co., 6 Misc 3d 1010(A), 800 NYS2d 341 (Civ. Ct. Kings Co. 2005); Capri Medical, P.C. v. Auto One Insurance Company, 14 Misc 3d 1205(A), 836 NYS2d 483 (Civ.Ct. Kings Co. 2006)

II. DEFENDANT'S DEFECTIVE FOLLOW-UP VERIFICATION REQUEST

DISSIPATED DEFENDANT'S INITIAL VERIFICATION AB INITIO

 

14.  In order to effectively toll defendant's statutory requirement to pay or deny plaintiffs claim within thirty (30) days, defendant was required to issue proper and timely verification requests. Assuming, arguendo, defendant has demonstrated it timely and properly mailed an initial verification request, the burden is on defendant to further demonstrate it timely and properly mailed a follow-up request. Indeed, defendant must demonstrate that such follow-up verification request was mailed to "the applicant and such person's attorney," as failure to do so voids defendant's initial verification request ab initio, and ultimately results in defendant's denial of claim being untimely. See generally, Star Medical Services, P.C. v. Allstate Ins. Co., 5 Misc.3d 785 (N.Y. City Civ. Ct. 2004); Sea Side Medical, P.e. v. State Farm Mutual Auto Ins. Co., 12 Misc.3d 1127 (N.Y. City Civ. Ct. 2006). Specifically, defendant is required to comply with 11 NYCRR 65-3.6 (b), to wit:

 

[I]f any requested verifications has not been supplied to the insurer 30 calendar days after the original request, the insurer shall, within 10 calendar days, follow up with the party from whom the verification was requests. At the same time the insurer shall inform the applicant and such person's attorney of the reason(s) why the claim is delayed by identifying in writing the missing verification and the party from whom it was requested. Id. (Emphasis added.)

 

15.  In Star Medical Services, P.C. v. Allstate Ins. Co., 5 Misc.3d 785, (N.Y.C. Civ. Ct., 2004), plaintiff commenced an action against defendant to recover outstanding no-fault benefits. Plaintiff moved for summary judgment and defendant opposed, alleging it effectively tolled the statutory thirty day period by requesting additional verification. In finding defendant failed to effectively toll the thirty day period and granting plaintiff's motion for summary judgment, the court held:

 

In the case at bar, the claimant provider was not notified of the request for an IME, and the request to the injured party was mailed to the wrong address. The only time the regulations mention an attorney is when it requires a request for additional verification to be sent both to the

applicant and his attorney. The court views this notice requirement for an additional verification as a dual requirement, as with service under the CPLR and RP APL, meaning that the failure to notify the claimant in a proper manner negates the effect of the request. Because the notices were never sent to the driver assignor's correct address, the court finds that defendant failed to make a legally valid request for verification within the time period prescribed by the regulations, and therefore the 30-day requirement was not tolled and the denial was untimely. The court emphasizes that the attorney who received the request represents the assignor in his own personal action for damages, and does not represent the assignor with respect to plaintiffs claim for first-party benefits. Therefore, since the representation involves separate claims and lawsuits, the court cannot assume that mailing requests to this lawyer is appropriate in this matter, since the assignor might retain different counsel for the claim under scrutiny. It has been held that mailing pleadings to a party's attorney did not constitute valid service when there was no evidence that the party authorized the attorney to accept such pleadings. Id (Emphasis added.)

 

16.  . In the instant case, defendant's burden is clearly set forth under the regulation. It is well settled that where the terms of a statute are plain and unambiguous, the statute must be construed in accordance with its expressed terms and should be construed so as to effectuate the plain meaning of the words used. Patrolmen's Benevolent Assn. of City of N.Y. v. City of New York, 41 N.Y.2d 205 (1976); Zaldin v. Concord Hotel, 48 NN.Y.2d 107 (1979); Matter of Peterson v. Daystrom Corp., 17 N.Y.2d 32 (1966); Matter of Carr v. New York State Bd. Of Elections, 40 N.Y.2d 556 (1976); Bender v. Jamaica Hosp., 40 N.Y.2d 560 (1976); New Amsterdam Cas. Co. v. Stecker, 3 N.Y.2d 1 (1957); Hyatt v. Taylor, 42 N.Y. 258 (1869). "The legislative intentis to be ascertained from the words and language used, and the statutory language is generally construed according to its natural and most obvious sense, without resorting to an artificial or forced construction" (McKinney'S Cons Laws ofN.Y., Book 1, Statutes, § 94).

 

17.  Therefore, in the instant case defendant had a clear duty to send its follow-up verification requests not only to the applicant, but to the applicant's attorney as well. Defendant's motion makes no mention of attempting to notify the applicant's attorney, coupled with the fact that defendant's affidavit's only attest to the mailing of such verification requests to the applicant. In addition, Plaintiff s attorney to date has no records of any verification requests received on file. Therefore, failure to notify the applicant's attorney as mentioned above voids defendant's initial verification request ab initio, and ultimately results in defendant's denial of claim being untimely.

 

18.  Accordingly, defendant has failed to properly toll the thirty (30) day rule and is precluded from asserting most affirmative defenses. As such, plaintiff humbly requests that the Court summarily deny defendant's motion in its entirety.
