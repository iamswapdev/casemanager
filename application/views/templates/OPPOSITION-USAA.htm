Alek Beynenson, an attorney duly admitted to practice law before the Courts of the State of New York; hereby affirms the following to be true under the penalties of perjury:

1.      I am a member of the law firm of The Beynenson Law Firm PC, attorneys of record for Plaintiff, and as such am fully, familiar with the facts and circumstances surrounding this action from the records and files maintained therein.

2.      This affirmation is submitted in opposition to Defendant's motion for Summary Judgment dismissing plaintiff’s complaint based on alleged fraudulent misrepresentations.

 

DEFENDANT'S MOTION IS DEFECTIVE AS A MATTER OF LAW

3.      Defendant contends that plaintiff’s assignor is not covered under any policy of insurance issued by the defendant.  In support of its contention defendant annexed an affidavit from its “litigation manager,” April Lally. Plaintiff respectfully submits defendant's affidavit is conclusory and defective as a matter of law. Specifically, April Lally contends the defendant only issues insurance policies to a certain demographic.  The criteria for eligibility includes, inter alia, active and former military personnel, children of parents who have or had a USAA auto or property insurance product, officer candidates in commissioning programs (Academy, ROTC, etc.), former USAA members who had USAA auto or property insurance, etc.

4.      April Lally further contends that applicant for the insurance policy at issue here allegedly indicated in an online application that she was “in US Coast Guard.” The affiant concedes that Defendant does not have a copy of that application.

5.      The affiant then states that “an online search [conducted by USAA] of the Service Members Civil Relief Act data banks … returned no results for Nakia Powell.”  The results of the search specifically indicate that the results are based on the “current status of the individual as to the all branches of Uniformed Services (Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Air Force, NOAA, Public Health, and Coast Guard).” (emphasis added) Exhibit A. 

6.      However, there is nothing establishing the Service Members Civil Relief Act data banks as definitively containing data for each and every person who meets USAA eligibility requirements.  For example, there is nothing indicating that the search is meant to return results of retired or former military personnel who are explicitly eligible for USAA insurance products.  It’s possible that the insured in this case qualified as a member of a commissioning program such as Academy or ROTC.  It is also possible that the assignee in this case qualified as a child of a qualifying USAA parent.

7.      The defendant failed to demonstrate that it performed an “exhaustive search.” Highlands Ins. Co. v. Baez, 18 A.D.3d 238 (1st Dept. 2005).

8.      In Baez, Highland Insurance Company alleged that it performed an "exhaustive search" of its computer system in support of its contention that coverage did not exist for a specific individual. In determining an "exhaustive search" was not performed, the Appellate Division held:

Appellant attempted to rebut this showing with the testimony of an employee that a computer search of appellant's records, using the policy number and name of the offending vehicle's owner as listed in the police report provided by the claimant, failed to turn up an auto policy insuring a person by that name and several variants thereof. This was less than the "exhaustive search" required to shift the burden back to petitioner to produce additional evidence of coverage (cf. Matter of American Tr. Ins. Co. [Glaude]. 208 A.D.2d 376, 377, 617 N.Y.S.2d 7 [1994] ). No reason appears why appellant did not also search its records for the owner's address and telephone number, or the offending vehicle's model type and license plate number, all information that was provided in the police report and typically provided in insurance applications and entered into appellant's computer. Id. at 239.

9.      Similarly, in the instant case, defendant merely searched its computer system based upon the insured’s name. Clearly, this was not an "exhaustive search" as defendant failed to search by other eligibility criteria it set out. See, Highlands, supra. Accordingly, defendant's motion must be denied.

  

WHEREFORE, plaintiff respectfully requests this Honorable Court issue an order denying defendant’s motion and for any other relief Honorable Court deems just and proper.
